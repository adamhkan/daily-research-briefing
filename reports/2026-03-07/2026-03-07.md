## Executive Summary

- Dexterous and bimanual manipulation see strong sim-to-real advances via tactile latent distillation and massive synthetic grasp datasets, significantly boosting in-hand and dual-arm performance.
- GPU-native motion generation and new sampling-based/planning algorithms (cuRoboV2, Many-RRT*, GAIDE, CDSM) push high-DoF and joint-space motion planning toward real-time, higher success, and lower-cost trajectories.
- Long-horizon and memory-intensive manipulation is tackled with new diffusion-policy variants and VLA frameworks (SeedPolicy, VPWEM, HD-ExpIt, RoboMME, Tri-System, Risk-aware mobile manipulation).
- Whole-body humanoid control and large-workspace manipulation benefit from physics-aware VLA control and omnidirectional LiDAR-based visuomotor policies.
- Safety, robustness, and uncertainty handling see progress through risk-aware RL, uncertainty-aware shared autonomy, distributionally robust collision avoidance, and contact-tolerant planning.
- Stanford and ETH contribute tools for interpreting and benchmarking VLA policies and for general, language-grounded navigation in open environments.

| PTLD: Sim-to-real Privileged Tactile Latent Distillation for Dexterous Manipulation | Carnegie Mellon University; UC Berkeley; University of Washington | Introduces PTLD, a sim-to-real framework that avoids tactile simulation by using privileged real-world tactile data to train a latent state estimator, which is then fused with proprioceptive policies trained in simulation. On in-hand rotation and reorientation benchmarks, incorporating PTLD yields large performance gains (up to 182% over proprioception-only baselines), demonstrating a practical path to tactile dexterous manipulation without high-fidelity tactile simulators. | https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.04531 |
| RoboMME: Benchmarking and Understanding Memory for Robotic Generalist Policies | Stanford University | Proposes RoboMME, a standardized benchmark of 16 manipulation tasks structured around temporal, spatial, object, and procedural memory requirements for VLA models. The authors systematically compare 14 memory-augmented variants of a π0.5-based policy, showing that memory design is highly task-dependent and providing a principled testbed for long-horizon, history-dependent manipulation. | https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.04639 |
| Observing and Controlling Features in Vision-Language-Action Models | Stanford University | Develops tools to probe and steer internal representations of VLA policies such as π0.5 and OpenVLA via linear feature observability and minimal optimal-control-based interventions. Experiments show that targeted modifications of internal features can reliably alter robot behavior online while preserving closed-loop performance, enabling real-time alignment without full fine-tuning. | https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05487 |
| OpenFrontier: General Navigation with Visual-Language Grounded Frontiers | ETH Zurich | Introduces OpenFrontier, a training-free navigation framework that selects frontier regions as visual anchors and scores them using off-the-shelf vision–language models to satisfy high-level language goals. The method avoids dense 3D mapping and policy training yet achieves strong zero-shot performance across benchmarks and real-world runs on a mobile robot. | https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05377 |
